Anxiety Lab Members

Graduate students

Emily Juel, M.A (she/her)

Emily Juel is a second-year doctoral student in the Clinical Psychology program. She grew up in Chapel Hill and earned her B.S. in Psychology and Sociology from Davidson College in 2021. After graduation, she worked as a postgraduate associate at the Yale Child Study Center in the Anxiety and Mood Disorders Program. Her work supported two federally funded clinical trials: one assessing the efficacy of a parent-based approach to reduce family accommodation in childhood anxiety disorders, and another examining a computer-based treatment for adolescents with social anxiety. Emily’s current research examines how parental accommodation behaviors contribute to the maintenance of anxiety and related disorders in young adults. She is particularly interested in how smartphone use, including family tracking apps and social media, may reinforce these patterns.

Joe Friedman, M.A. (he/him)

Joe is a 3rd year graduate student from Pittsboro, NC. He received his B.A. in psychology from the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Before graduate school, he worked as the research coordinator of the Boundaries of Anxiety and Depression Lab at the University of Pennsylvania. His research interests include cognitive biases associated with anxiety and related disorders, and exposure-based intervention strategies. He recently defended his master's thesis on predictors of posttraumatic stress symptoms. Outside of the lab, Joe enjoys hiking, biking, and barbecue.

Nick Myers, M.A. (he/him)

Nick is a fourth [fifth year starting this Fall] year doctoral student in the UNC Clinical Psychology program. He was born and raised in rural Maryland. Nick earned a B.S. in Psychology and a M.A. in Clinical Psychology from Towson University in Baltimore, Maryland. Prior to coming to UNC, Nick worked full-time as a clinical research coordinator at the Johns Hopkins Center for OCD, Anxiety, and Related Disorders for Children (JH-COACH). At UNC, Nick’s primary areas of research include investing cognitive-behavioral models of OCD and the dimensional structure of OCD symptom presentations.
